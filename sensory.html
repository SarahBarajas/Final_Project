Individuals with autism often experience unique sensory processing challenges,
which can significantly impact their daily lives. Sensory sensitivity in autism
can manifest as either hypersensitivity (over-responsiveness) or hyposensitivity (under-responsiveness)
to sensory stimuli such as sounds, textures, tastes, and lights1. For example, a person with hypersensitivity might
find everyday noises unbearably loud or certain fabrics extremely uncomfortable,
leading to anxiety and avoidance behaviors2.
Conversely, someone with hyposensitivity might seek out intense sensory experiences,
such as spinning or touching various textures, to fulfill their sensory needs

ability to participate in social activities and daily routines.
For example, a child with auditory hypersensitivity might struggle in a noisy classroom,
leading to difficulties in focusing and learning. Similarly, someone with tactile sensitivities
might avoid certain activities, such as playing with sand or finger painting, due to the
discomfort caused by these textures. These sensory differences can also impact personal
care routines; for instance, brushing teeth or hair might be distressing due to the sensations
involved. Understanding and accommodating these sensory needs is crucial in creating supportive
environments that allow individuals with autism to thrive.
